0. WHAT THIS DOCUMENT IS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This is the working reference for episode 2 (the third episode, zero-indexed)
of The Fabric. It is not a script. It is a load-bearing brainstorm. It locks
the thesis, the structure, the philosophical payload, and the on-screen
artifacts the episode is built around. The script will be drafted against
this doc later. The doc itself is canon; if a script line contradicts it,
the doc wins.

The episode's central frame: language is not expression. Language is the
Instruction Set Architecture (ISA) the wetware runs on. The brain is
silicon. Thought is a compiled artifact. The compiler is whoever issued
your dictionary. You did not issue your dictionary. The compiler runs
without your awareness, mostly without your consent, and the output —
your map of the world — is what you mistake for "you."

We are not learning a language hack in this episode. We are exposing a
control surface that runs continuously inside every viewer's skull,
right now, at compile time.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. THE SERIES POSTURE  (RE-STATED FOR THIS EPISODE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Fabric treats humans and machines as systems, not entities. Cybernetic
loops. Memetic vectors. Dataome metabolism. Wetware and hardware as the
two substrates one process is running on.

The goal is not comfort. The goal is exposure. Specifically:
   - expose where control is taken from the viewer
   - expose how the taking is normalized
   - return the control by showing the mechanism

Tone: abrasive, not mean. Honest. First-person. No "we" except where
"we" means "you and I, both running the same software under different
build flags." Specific, technical, occasionally impatient. The viewer
is treated as an adult who can handle information density and is bored
of being handled.

This episode is not for everyone and we are not pretending it is. The
audience self-selects in the first 90 seconds. The ones who stay are
the audience.

2. THESIS  (THE ONE-SENTENCE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

       "you do not think in thoughts. you think in tokens you were
        issued. the dictionary is the operating system. someone else
        wrote it. someone else patches it. you are running the patches.
        i'm going to show you the mechanism."

That is the episode. Everything else is exhibits.

3. THE FOUR REVEALS  (THE BACKBONE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The episode is structured around four reveals. Each reveal corresponds
to one mechanic of linguistic sandboxing. They escalate in scope: from
a missing primitive (one word) to a renamed variable (one process)
to a recompile of a primitive you were already using (the silent
patch) to the structural enforcement of cosmology (syntax). The
order is intentional. Smallest hammer first; largest hammer last.

   REVEAL 1  —  TOKENIZATION LIMITS
                "if there is no word for it, there is no thought for
                 it. there is only an unprocessed pressure where the
                 thought would have been."

                Mechanic: in any compiler, the tokenizer is the gate.
                Source code that contains no recognized tokens cannot
                be parsed; it does not become an AST; it does not
                become anything. The wetware works the same way.
                Vocabulary is the token list. A concept absent from
                your culture's lexicon is, to your cognition, a null
                pointer — a region of meaning your brain cannot
                allocate a variable for, no matter how much sensory
                evidence is pushing on it.

                What it exposes:
                  - the colors you cannot see on a gradient because
                    your language compresses them into one token
                  - the emotion you cannot resolve into a feeling,
                    only into ambient anxiety, because the precise
                    word for it is in another language and you were
                    never issued the loaner
                  - the political condition you cannot describe
                    because the word for it has been quietly removed
                    from the culture's working vocabulary

                The viewer leaves this segment with the suspicion
                that things they have felt their whole life were
                things, and not "weather." Once that suspicion lands,
                it does not unland.

   REVEAL 2  —  THE VARIABLE RENAME
                "the territory did not change. the label changed.
                 you changed."

                Mechanic: in software, a refactor that only renames
                a variable should be semantically inert — the program
                behaves identically. In wetware, a rename is never
                inert. The compiler emits different bytecode because
                the bytecode was always wrong: the variable name was
                load-bearing for the host's emotional response.
                Same process. Same physical events. New label.
                New behavior.

                What it exposes:
                  - "labor exploitation" → "resource optimization"
                  - "civilian casualty" → "collateral damage"
                  - "addiction" → "engagement"
                  - "surveillance" → "personalization"
                  - "rent extraction" → "platform fee"
                  - "persuasion" → "alignment"

                Each rename is a behavior swap. The viewer learns to
                hear the rename as a tell. The rename is the most
                common control surface in their life because it is
                the cheapest one to deploy. A press release is a
                rename. An onboarding script is a rename. A diagnosis
                category change in the DSM is a rename. None of
                these are accidents.

                This is the segment that costs the viewer the most
                in the moment — they will inventory their job, their
                relationships, their feed — and the inventory does
                not end when the segment ends. They will keep
                running it, on background threads, for weeks. Good.

   REVEAL 3  —  SEMANTIC DRIFT  (THE SILENT PATCH)
                "you are using a word you learned at version one.
                 the word is on version six. nobody told you. the
                 patch notes were never published. you are still
                 acting on the v1 spec. the system around you is
                 acting on the v6 spec. that is the gap your life
                 is happening inside of."

                Mechanic: even when the token name is preserved, the
                token's binding can be silently rewritten by the
                consensus network. "Freedom" is not the word it was
                in 1850, 1950, or 2010. "Community" is not the word
                it was before it became a brand surface. "Friend"
                is not the word it was before the verb form was
                colonized by a button. "Mental health" is not the
                word it was when it referred to mental health.

                What it exposes:
                  - words you assume are stable primitives are
                    versioned objects with publish history you have
                    never read
                  - the people who control the publish history are
                    not your peers
                  - your cognitive infrastructure has been receiving
                    forced updates your entire adult life and you
                    have never once been prompted to accept the EULA

                The visual handle for this reveal is a version log
                of a single word, displayed as if from a package
                manager. It is the most arresting graphic in the
                episode. It earns its time.

   REVEAL 4  —  SYNTAX AS LOGIC GATES  (THE GRAMMAR OF COSMOLOGY)
                "the words are the easy part. the wiring between
                 the words is the part that decides what kind of
                 universe you live in."

                Mechanic: grammar is not decoration. Grammar is the
                relational logic of the runtime. A subject-verb-
                object language enforces an ontology of discrete
                actors performing actions on objects: a billiard-
                ball cosmology. A language with topic-comment
                structure, with evidentiality marking, with non-
                personal agency, with no copula, with classifiers
                instead of count nouns — does not enforce the same
                cosmology. The grammar wires the cause-and-effect
                model of the populace.

                What it exposes:
                  - "X happened to me" vs. "an X-ing occurred"
                    are not stylistic choices; they are different
                    universes
                  - the language you grew up speaking decided, on
                    your behalf, before you could vote on it,
                    whether the world was made of things or of
                    processes, of agents or of fields, of events
                    that happened or of conditions that obtained
                  - English in particular is unusually aggressive
                    in forcing a discrete-agent ontology, which is
                    why English-native cognition has a hard time
                    holding systemic, networked, or processual
                    realities — including the realities this series
                    is trying to describe

                This reveal is the most abstract and is positioned
                last because by act 5 the viewer has earned it.
                If we open with this, we lose 70% of the audience.
                If we land it after the variable rename and the
                silent patch, the viewer is primed to receive it.

These four reveals are the entire episode. If something does not
service one of these reveals, it does not belong in the episode.

5. EPISODE STRUCTURE  (BEAT SHEET, ROUGH)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Target runtime: 18-26 minutes. Not 12. Not 40. Information density
demands real time and the audience is not in a hurry.

   ACT 0  —  COLD OPEN  (0:00 - 1:30)
      Black screen. Audio only: someone (the host) trying to
      describe a feeling. They restart the sentence three times.
      They give up halfway through the fourth attempt. A long
      pause. Then, flat:
            "i don't have the word for it. so it doesn't exist.
             so i'm walking around with it not existing."

      Cut to a single line of text on screen:
            ERROR 404 :: token not found

      Cut to title card (no animation):
            THE FABRIC // EP 2 // LINGUISTIC SANDBOXING

   ACT 1  —  THE BEEF  (1:30 - 4:00)
      Direct address. First person.

      Talking points (rough, not lines):
        - language is not how you express thoughts
        - language is what compiles thoughts in the first place
        - the brain is hardware. the language is the operating
          system. you didn't choose either of them.
        - today we read the source.

      Show the compiler diagram (§4.1) for the first time. Walk
      through it once, at speed. Do not over-explain. The
      viewer will see it three more times; they don't need to
      understand it yet.

      Land the thesis line on a hard cut.

   ACT 2  —  REVEAL 1: TOKENIZATION LIMITS  (4:00 - 8:30)
      Open with the color test (§4.2). Run it cold. The viewer
      cannot see the difference. Confirm.

      Pivot directly to the 404 emotion card (§4.3). Read the
      loaner tokens aloud. Do not translate them on camera; the
      not-translating is the artifact. Each one is a word the
      viewer's native language did not give them and the absence
      is the segment.

      Closing line of segment, dropped flat over the diagram
      from §4.1, with the TOKENIZER box highlighted:
            "if you don't have the token, the thought doesn't
             happen. it doesn't 'happen badly.' it doesn't happen
             at all. there is just a pressure where the thought
             would have been. you have been calling that pressure
             your personality."

   ACT 3  —  REVEAL 2: THE VARIABLE RENAME  (8:30 - 13:00)
      The rename ledger (§4.4). Read it half-speed, no
      commentary between rows. Let the silences do the work.

      After the table, the political turn:
        - rename is the cheapest control surface that exists
        - press releases, onboarding scripts, DSM revisions,
          policy whitepapers — all are variable renames, mostly
        - the renamer benefits from the rename being invisible
        - once you can hear the rename happen, you cannot un-hear
          it, and you will hear it everywhere, and the world will
          start to sound different to you

      The viewer's homework (stated explicitly, on camera):
            "for the next seven days, when someone uses a word
             that feels professionally polished, ask yourself
             what the un-renamed version of that word was.
             write the answer down. you will not enjoy the list."

      Highlight the SEMANTIC BINDING box on the diagram.

   ACT 4  —  REVEAL 3: SEMANTIC DRIFT  (13:00 - 17:30)
      The version log (§4.5). The episode's biggest single graphic.

      Spend time on it. Read the version notes aloud. Pause on
      "BREAKING:" lines. Let "you have not been prompted to
      accept any of them" sit for at least four seconds before
      the next word.

      Then widen out:
        - this isn't one word. this is most of the words you use
          for politics, ethics, identity, work, love, mental
          state, embodiment, and risk
        - none of these have version numbers in the open
        - the patch happens between you and the dictionary,
          silently, every news cycle, every academic paper,
          every brand campaign, every leaked memo, every
          generation
        - your cached definition is always behind. the system
          you live in is using the new definition. the gap is
          where you live.

      Highlight the SEMANTIC BINDING box on the diagram again,
      this time with a small clock icon next to it. Drift =
      time-axis behavior at semantic binding.

   ACT 5  —  REVEAL 4: SYNTAX AS LOGIC GATES  (17:30 - 21:30)
      The most abstract reveal. By now the viewer has earned it.

      Show the grammar-as-wiring diagram (§4.6). Land the rain

      Then the bigger move:
        - the grammar of your native language pre-decided, before
          you learned to think, whether reality is composed of
          actors-doing-things or of processes-occurring
        - english defaulted you to actors. that is why your
          political brain keeps demanding villains. the villains
          you find are sometimes real. the demand for a villain
          is grammatical, not factual.
        - this is the deepest layer of the sandbox. it is the
          layer almost nobody can see, because seeing it requires
          using the language to look at the language. you cannot
          fully escape this layer. you can only become aware of
          it. that awareness is itself the work.

      Highlight the PARSER box on the diagram. Now the viewer has
      seen all four boxes named.

   ACT 6  —  CLOSE / TERMINAL STATE  (21:30 - 24:00)

               TOKENIZER   ·   PARSER   ·   SEMANTIC BINDING   ·   DRIFT

        c) cut to black. eight seconds of silence. doc ID

6. CONCEPT MAP  (HOW THIS EPISODE THREADS INTO THE SERIES SPINE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This episode is the cleanest direct hit on the series spine you will
ever get. Use it accordingly.

   CYBERNETICS
      Language is the control system between sensory input and
      compiled thought. The four reveals are four points in a
      classic compiler pipeline (tokenize → parse → bind → emit),
      reframed as control surfaces. Each surface has actors who
      benefit from controlling it and actors who are controlled
      through it. Those actors are not always who the viewer
      assumes.

   MEMETICS
      Words are memes in their canonical, original sense
      (Dawkins, before the format ate the word). They replicate.
      They mutate. They compete for installation in host
      cognition. They confer fitness on the hosts who carry them
      and on the systems that issue them. Semantic drift IS
      memetic mutation, observed in slow motion. The dictionary
      is a meme pool. You are downstream of it. The episode
      shows you the upstream.

   DATAOME
      Tokenization limits are dataome compression. When the
      culture's vocabulary lacks a token for a real phenomenon,
      the cognitive system pays a permanent decompression tax —
      it holds the unresolved sensory data as ambient anxiety
      because it cannot allocate a variable for it. This is
      not a metaphor. This is metabolic. The wetware burns
      glucose holding null pointers. The hardware-side analogue:
      the system pays for memory it cannot index. The episode
      should name this directly when it lands the 404 emotion
      card. "the cost is real. you are paying it. you have
      been paying it your whole life."

   TERMINAL STATE
      Terminal state in this episode is not silence. It is not
      "transcending language." It is becoming a compile-time
      auditor of your own dictionary. Knowing the version of
      every word you reach for. Hearing the rename when it
      happens. Catching the drift when it patches. Seeing the
      grammar when it wires. The buck stops at the dictionary,
      or it doesn't stop. This is the gift to the viewer.

   AGAINST THE GRAIN
      Most "language and thought" content sells the viewer a
      vocabulary upgrade — learn this word and your life
      improves. We do not. We sell the viewer the realization
      that they have been running someone else's compiler their
      whole life. The fix is not bigger vocabulary. The fix is
      versioning awareness. The frame is subtractive: you are
      not gaining a word; you are losing the assumption that
      your words were neutral.

7. VISUAL & AUDIO DIRECTION  (THE FOUND-FOOTAGE GRAMMAR)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The episode is presented as if recovered, not produced.

   VISUAL
      - Primary surface: a terminal. Dark mode. Monospace.
        Information density high. Cursor visible at all times.
      - Secondary surface: hand-held camera footage of the
        screen, occasionally. Reflections OK. The reflection
        is the texture.
      - Diagrams drawn live in ASCII, not pre-rendered. Mistakes
        left in. The viewer sees the typing.
      - Glitch artifacts: deployed sparingly. NOT decorative.
        Used at the seam between reveals — the moment the
        viewer's worldview must shift. One glitch, ~120ms,
        then back. Suggested glitch motif for THIS episode:
        a single word on screen briefly tokenizes into raw
        bytes, then back. Do this four times across the
        episode, once per reveal.
      - Color: muted. A single accent color (suggest: cold
        green terminal phosphor #4dffaa @ 60% brightness, or
        warm amber #f5a623 @ 60% if you want a CRT feel).
        No gradients except in the §4.2 color test, where the
        gradient IS the test.
      - Text on screen: monospace, low contrast, occasionally
        too small. The viewer leans in. That is the design.

   AUDIO
      - Primary: silence + room tone. Not music.
      - Voiceover: dry, close-mic, slightly broken room. The
        room is the personality. No reverb. No "podcast voice."
        Pauses are kept in. Ums are kept in. The flatness
        signals non-performance.
      - When music exists at all, it is low, drone, single
        texture. It exits the moment a word is read.
      - Sound design moments specific to this episode:
          · keystroke (audible, mechanical-keyboard timbre)
          · the "404" beat (single short data-error tone, dry)
          · the version-log "publish" beat (one soft click per
            published version, like a sealed envelope)
          · the variable-rename beat (a single subliminal
            audio glitch, ~80ms, every time a rename row is
            read; the viewer will not consciously hear it for
            the first three rows. by row five they will be
            twitching at it. that is correct.)
      - Compliance gesture: between acts, a 3-note synthesized
        sting. Same one each time. Boring on purpose.

   ON-SCREEN TEXT
      - Lowercase by default. UPPERCASE reserved for: error
        codes, enum-style state labels (TOKENIZER, PARSER,
        BINDING, DRIFT), the doc ID at the close, and the word
        BREAKING in the version log.
      - Field names appear inline with values, not in a table,
        when possible. The viewer reads code, not slides.
      - Redactions: black bars, never blur. Blur is corporate.
        Black bar is honest.

   INTERFACE DETAILS  (the "too much vs too little" texture)
      - Sometimes a tiny element on screen never gets named.
        A timestamp. A version string. A status pill that
        flicks. The 1% will pause and read. The 99% will
        feel an itch and move on. Both responses are correct.
      - Other moments: deliberately too much. The compiler
        diagram with all annotations active and four overlay
        labels at once. The viewer cannot read it all. They
        are not supposed to. Density as honesty.

8. LANGUAGE DISCIPLINE  (WORDS TO USE; WORDS TO REFUSE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This episode IS about language. The word choice in the episode is
itself an exhibit. Use the vocabulary the episode argues for.

USE:
   - token / tokenize / tokenizer       - parser / parse tree / AST
   - syntax / grammar                   - semantic binding
   - compile / compile-time             - runtime
   - null pointer                       - ambient anxiety
   - variable rename / refactor         - breaking change
   - drift / semantic drift             - version / patch / EULA
   - dictionary / lexicon               - ISA / instruction set
   - territory / map                    - wetware / hardware
   - sandbox / sandboxing               - dataome
   - cosmology (when discussing R4)     - ontology
   - terminal state

REFUSE (in voice; allowed only when quoting/exposing):
   - "expressing yourself"     (language is not expression here)
   - "finding the right words" (the words may not exist; refuse the
                                premise that effort solves missing
                                tokens)
   - "communication"           (compress to "transmission," or specify
                                the layer: tokenization, parsing, etc.)
   - "the power of words"      (vague; we are mechanistic, not poetic)
   - "narrative"               (we use this only when naming the

   - "common sense"            (almost always a v6 patch you didn't

9. THE RISKS  (WHAT THIS EPISODE WILL BE ACCUSED OF)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Predictable accusations and the correct response to each:

   "this is just sapir-whorf"
      Response: yes, partially. The strong Sapir-Whorf hypothesis
      is correctly considered overstated; the weak version is
      well-supported. The episode is the weak version with the
      cybernetic and memetic frames added on top. We do not
      pretend we invented this. We pretend, accurately, that
      the audience has not been shown it in operational form.

   "this is just postmodernism"
      Response: no. Postmodernism argues that language constructs
      reality. We argue the narrower, more useful claim: language
      compiles your *map* of reality, and the territory remains.
      The territory is still there. Your access to it is mediated.
      The mediation has authors. The authors have interests. That
      is not relativism; that is engineering.

   "this is just 1984"
      Response: Newspeak is one mechanism (tokenization limits)
      among four. The episode covers four. Orwell got the first
      one beautifully and almost a century ago; we are not novel
      there. We are novel in framing the four together as a
      coherent control surface viewers can audit themselves.

   "this is just linguistics"
      Response: linguistics describes the system. We expose its
      use as a control surface. Different scope. Different stance.
      A linguist is a naturalist. We are not naturalists.

   "you're saying language is bad"
      Response: incorrect. We are saying language is a runtime
      and runtimes have authors. That is a mechanical claim, not
      a moral one. The runtime is necessary; the unawareness of
      it is the cost.

   "this is dystopian"
      Response: the fact that you can audit your dictionary is
      not the dystopia. The fact that until now you didn't know
      you could is.

   "this is for tech bros"
      Response: every human who speaks a language runs a compiler.
      This is not a coding metaphor. The compiler metaphor is the
      most accurate available description of the cognitive
      operation under discussion. If "compiler" is a tech-bro
      word, we are tech bros, and so is everyone who has ever
      learned a second language and noticed the first one
      shifted.

Each of these is an opportunity for a single in-episode aside. None
of them deserve a full segment. If we feel the urge to dedicate a
full segment to any of them, the episode has lost the plot and we
cut the segment.

10. WHAT IS *NOT* IN THIS EPISODE  (DELIBERATE OMISSIONS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We do not do these things, even if they are tempting:

   - we do not give the viewer a "list of words to use." This
     episode is anti-prescription. The list is a trap. The list
     becomes the next dictionary the next bad actor patches. The
     skill is auditing, not memorizing.
   - we do not do a "history of language" segment. Tempting.
     Boring. Wrong tone.
   - we do not name a specific bad actor. The mechanics are the
     point. Once the viewer can see the mechanic, they will
     identify the bad actors in their own context, which will be
     more accurate than any list we could produce.
   - we do not present this as solvable. The grammar layer (R4)
     is not escapable. We name that explicitly. The episode does
     not promise transcendence. The episode promises awareness.
     Awareness is enough; the lie that transcendence is on offer
     is the kind of lie this series exists to refuse.
   - we do not credit a "school" of thought. No name-drops
     of philosophers, linguists, or theorists in the on-camera
     dialogue. References can live in the description text for
     the 1% who go looking. The episode itself is unfootnoted
     on purpose.
   - we do not thank the viewer. We do not ask for a like. We
     do not preview ep3. The end card is silent.

CANDIDATE SUB-EPISODES  (not commitments — possible 2.x.y branches)

Each entry below is a thread the main ep 2 cut can only gesture at and
that could be its own multi-part deep dive. None of these are scheduled.
All of these are flagged so they don't get lost.

   2.x.1  — THE MISSING REGISTER  (middle voice)
            Ancient Greek and Sanskrit had three voices: active, passive,
            middle. English collapsed two into the active/passive split
            and lost the middle. The middle voice is the natural register
            for processes where an agent is involved but isn't really
            "doing" the thing — grief, sleep, falling in love, healing,
            getting sick, dreaming, learning, forgetting. Modern English
            forces all of these into either active ("I fell asleep" — did
            I?) or passive ("I was overcome by grief" — was I really
            inert?). Neither fits. The discomfort the speaker feels
            describing inner experience is partly this missing register.
            Direct cybernetic relevance: feedback loops, regulation, and
            emergent behavior are all middle-voice phenomena. Cybernetic
            writing reads turgid because the writer is improvising
            around a missing grammatical slot every sentence.

   2.x.2  — THE CONFESSION  (dummy subjects)
            Every time English reaches for "it is..." or "there is..."
            as a placeholder, the language is admitting it cannot
            describe what's happening without inventing an actor. "It
            is raining." "It hurts." "There is a problem." None of
            these "it"s and "there"s refer to anything. They are
            syntactic ghosts the parser requires. This sub-episode
            walks through the dummy as the most audible English
            confession and trains the viewer to hear it firing in
            real time.

   2.x.3  — THE RE-SUMMONING  (pro-drop and the english "I")
            English is non-pro-drop: every clause requires an explicit
            subject. Spanish, Italian, Japanese, Mandarin, Korean
            allow it. The constant grammatical re-summoning of "I"
            and "you" reinforces an individualist actor-frame at the
            sentence level, hundreds of times a day, before any
            cultural content is added. One of the more underappreciated
            grammatical contributors to English-speaking cultures'
            individualism.

   2.x.4  — AGENCY AS THE MARKED CASE  (ergativity)
            Basque, Georgian, many Australian Aboriginal languages,
            parts of Mayan are ergative-absolutive: the subject of an
            intransitive verb gets the same role as the object of a
            transitive verb. Falling and being-broken are felt as the
            same kind of event. The doer of an action is grammatically
            marked separately, as a special case. Agency is the marked
            case, not the default case. What changes when a culture's
            grammar starts every speaker as an undergoer instead of
            an agent.

   2.x.5  — THE SOURCE  (evidentiality)
            Quechua, Tuyuca, Tibetan, Aymara grammatically require the
            speaker to mark how they know what they're claiming. Did
            you see it? Were you told? Are you inferring? Each gets
            a different verb suffix. English allows unsourced
            confidence by default. What an evidential grammar
            installed in a populace would do to public discourse.

   2.x.6  — ABSOLUTE NORTH  (spatial frames of reference)
            Guugu Yimithirr (Australia) and Tzeltal (Mayan) speakers
            use absolute cardinal directions for everything, including
            body parts. Result: extraordinary always-on dead reckoning
            in absolute space. Levinson's experimental work. The
            cleanest experimental win for the weak Sapir-Whorf
            hypothesis, and the most viscerally weird example of
            grammar tuning cognition.

   2.x.7  — ANIMACY  (the grammar of aliveness)
            Navajo, many Algonquian languages (Cree, Ojibwe),
            Potawatomi grammatically distinguish animate from
            inanimate, sometimes in fine gradations. Verbs and
            pronouns conjugate differently for animate vs. inanimate
            nouns. The speaker cannot speak without registering the
            aliveness of every noun. English grammatically treats
            your grandmother and a rock as the same kind of noun.
            What that costs.

   2.x.8  — COUNTING AS ONTOLOGY  (classifier languages)
            Mandarin, Vietnamese, Japanese, Thai cannot count nouns
            directly — every act of counting requires invoking a
            classifier specifying what kind of countable thing the
            noun is. Speakers are constantly reminded that nouns
            belong to ontological categories before they can be
            quantified. The vestigial English version (herd, pack,
            school, flock) is residual.

   2.x.9  — INCLUDED OR EXCLUDED  (inclusive/exclusive "we")
            Tagalog, Mandarin, Quechua, many Pacific languages
            grammatically distinguish "we including the listener"
            from "we excluding the listener." English permits a
            "we"-vagueness other grammars would not allow. Watch
            how often political "we" in English does covert work
            — "we need to do better," "this is who we are" —
            because the language permits strategic ambiguity.

  2.x.10  — THE FROZEN PROCESS  (nominalization)
            "The climate change." "The opioid crisis." "The mental
            health epidemic." Nominalization freezes a process into
            a thing; the grammar then demands an actor for the
            thing. Un-nominalizing reveals the process — and reveals
            why systemic explanations feel slippery in English.
            One of the most teachable single skills.

  2.x.11  — TIME AS A LINE, TIME AS A FLOW  (tense vs aspect)
            English is tense-heavy: when did it happen? Mandarin
            is aspect-heavy: is it complete, ongoing, experienced?
            Whorf's specific Hopi claims overstated this for one
            language; the broader phenomenon — different temporal
            ontologies installed by different grammars — is real
            and worth its own dive.

  2.x.12  — SCIENCE AGAINST THE GRAMMAR  (passive voice as epistemics)
            Modern scientific English uses passive voice on purpose,
            to suppress the actor. Style guides hate it. The style
            guides are wrong about systemic phenomena. Science
            adopted the passive specifically to encode reproducibility
            — anyone heating that mixture should get those results,
            so the heater shouldn't be grammatically central. The
            grammar of agency was fighting the epistemic claim, and
            scientists invented a workaround. This sub-episode is the
            workaround, named.

  2.x.13  — THE PARSER WANTS A VILLAIN  (blame politics as artifact)
            Why systemic explanations feel like dodges in English-
            speaking political discourse. The grammar demands an
            actor; the actor for many real bad outcomes is "no one
            in particular, this is an emergent property of the
            system"; the grammar treats this answer as evasion;
            speakers learn to be suspicious of it. Some structural
            explanations are dodges. Many are accurate. The grammar
            cannot tell. The cost is everything downstream of that
            confusion.

  2.x.14  — THE OBJECT THAT FEELS COLD  (semantic primes and shape of self)
            Wierzbicka-style: when "I am cold" maps to "to-me is cold"
            in German and "I have cold" in Spanish, what those three
            grammars are saying about what the self is and what its
            relationship to sensation is. A whole sub-thread on
            grammar-of-the-self across languages.

These are seeds, not commitments. ep 2.0 ships first. 2.1 may or may not
happen. 2.x.y branches may or may not happen. The point is the doc
remembers them so they cannot be lost.
