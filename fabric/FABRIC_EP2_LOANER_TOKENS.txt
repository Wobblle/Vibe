WHAT THIS IS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A list of words from other languages with no clean English equivalent.
Each entry: the token, the source language, a brief gloss, and a single
usage line.

The list is not comprehensive. It is a starter. The point is not to
memorize this list. The point is to install one or two tokens that
resolve a feeling you currently carry as ambient anxiety, and let the
resolution show you what tokenization actually does inside your head.

If a token resolves something for you, the symbol is yours. Use it.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DISCLAIMERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

   - These are functional glosses, not certified translations. None
     of these will translate cleanly into English. That is the point.

   - Several tokens commonly cited in "untranslatable words" lists
     online are exaggerated or misattributed. The list below sticks
     to tokens that are well-attested in their source culture's
     literature or scholarship. A few entries are flagged where the
     scholarly gloss is contested.

   - Borrowing a token does not give you the cultural context that
     produced it. Using "ubuntu" or "han" outside their cultural
     contexts can be insensitive when done thoughtlessly. Borrow
     respectfully or use these as pointers to learn the surrounding
     culture.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOKENS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


saudade           [portuguese]
                  a longing for something or someone that may never
                  return, or that may never have been. distinct from
                  nostalgia: nostalgia is for a remembered place;
                  saudade is for an impossibility.
                  → "the saudade hit when i found the photograph
                     in the bottom of the box."


sehnsucht         [german]
                  a yearning for a kind of life or experience never
                  actually had. an ache for an idealized state outside
                  one's own history. discussed extensively by C.S.
                  Lewis in "Surprised by Joy" (1955) as a key emotional
                  category English does not capture.
                  → "the sehnsucht has been with me since the train
                     pulled into the station and i didn't get off."


hiraeth           [welsh]
                  a homesickness for a home that may not have existed,
                  or for a Wales that may have been imagined. central
                  category in Welsh literature and identity.
                  → "what he called hiraeth, his cousins recognized
                     immediately and his coworkers did not."


mono no aware     [japanese, 物の哀れ]
                  the soft sadness produced by noticing that things
                  end. an awareness of impermanence held without
                  resistance. central to Heian aesthetics; formalized
                  by Motoori Norinaga in the eighteenth century.
                  → "the cherry blossoms were mono no aware itself
                     and she only stopped to look for a moment."


toska             [russian, тоска]
                  a deep, unspecific spiritual ache. Vladimir Nabokov,
                  in his commentary on Eugene Onegin: "no single word
                  in English renders all the shades of toska."
                  → "the toska had no cause and no remedy and he had
                     learned to walk with it."


litost            [czech]
                  the specific torment of suddenly recognizing one's
                  own miserable condition. Milan Kundera defines it
                  formally in "The Book of Laughter and Forgetting"
                  (1979).
                  → "she was overcome with litost when she saw the
                     reflection in the storefront window."


duende            [spanish, esp. andalusian]
                  the dark, soulful quality that emerges in art at
                  moments of emotional truth. Federico García Lorca
                  lectured on it in 1933: "all that has black sounds
                  has duende."
                  → "the singer didn't have technique. she had duende.
                     the room knew it before the second verse."


yūgen             [japanese, 幽玄]
                  a profound awareness of the universe that triggers
                  feelings too deep and mysterious for words. central
                  to Noh aesthetics and classical Japanese poetry.
                  → "the silence after the bell was yūgen, and they
                     stayed in it for as long as they could."


wabi-sabi         [japanese, 侘寂]
                  an aesthetic of finding beauty in imperfection,
                  impermanence, and incompleteness. distinct from
                  "shabby chic" or the design-blog version.
                  → "the kintsugi bowl was wabi-sabi made literal:
                     the cracks were the point."


han               [korean, 한]
                  a collective, generationally-inherited sorrow and
                  unresolved resentment. complex cultural category
                  in Korean literature and historiography. contested
                  in scholarship: some argue han is constructed,
                  others that it predates the concept's modern
                  popularization. either way, it names something.
                  → "his grandmother said the han had passed through
                     three of their bodies before reaching his."


fernweh           [german]
                  the inverse of homesickness: an ache for places
                  one has never been. literally "far-sickness."
                  → "the fernweh started in march and the airline
                     credit ran out in june."


waldeinsamkeit    [german]
                  the feeling of being alone in the woods. specifically
                  the contemplative, slightly uncanny version of that
                  aloneness, not loneliness in the negative sense.
                  → "he went into the trees for the waldeinsamkeit
                     and came out three hours later, calmer."


vorfreude         [german]
                  the joy of anticipation. distinct from excitement:
                  vorfreude is the slow, savored state of looking
                  forward to something good.
                  → "the vorfreude was better than the trip turned
                     out to be, and that was fine."


schadenfreude     [german]
                  pleasure derived from another's misfortune. widely
                  borrowed into english but worth keeping on this
                  list because the absence of an organic English
                  word for it is itself diagnostic.
                  → "she felt the schadenfreude and then felt
                     ashamed of feeling it, which was its own
                     small punishment."


ubuntu            [nguni bantu, multiple southern african languages]
                  the principle that one's humanity is constituted
                  through relationships with others. "I am because
                  we are." central to southern African philosophical
                  traditions. note: borrowing this token without
                  understanding its philosophical depth flattens it.
                  → "the policy was anti-ubuntu by design and most
                     of the staff couldn't articulate why it felt
                     wrong."


sisu              [finnish]
                  a stoic, almost stubborn determination in the face
                  of sustained adversity. cultural keyword in Finnish
                  identity; cannot be performed, only demonstrated.
                  → "she didn't talk about sisu, which was how he
                     knew she had it."


meraki            [greek, μεράκι]
                  the soul, creativity, or love poured into doing
                  something. the part of oneself put into the work,
                  whether anyone notices or not.
                  → "the cabinet maker put meraki into joints nobody
                     would ever see, and that was the answer to most
                     questions you could ask about him."


hygge             [danish]
                  cozy contentment. a quality of warmth, ease, and
                  shared comfort. heavily commercialized in english-
                  language media; the original is more specific and
                  less aesthetic-driven.
                  → "the hygge was real that night, and disappeared
                     once the candles were photographed."


lagom             [swedish]
                  the right amount, not too much, not too little.
                  a quiet ethical orientation more than a quantity.
                  → "his portion was lagom, and he stopped eating
                     while the food was still good."


ikigai            [japanese, 生き甲斐]
                  the reason for being. that which gives one's life
                  meaning and motivation. heavily simplified in
                  popular english-language books into a four-circle
                  diagram; the original is messier.
                  → "after the diagnosis, he stopped pretending the
                     job was his ikigai and started looking for
                     what was."


komorebi          [japanese, 木漏れ日]
                  the sunlight that filters through leaves in a
                  forest canopy. a noun for a specific, observed
                  visual phenomenon English does not name.
                  → "they walked under the komorebi for an hour
                     and neither of them spoke."


tsundoku          [japanese, 積ん読]
                  the act of buying books and not reading them,
                  letting them pile up. acknowledged in Japanese
                  as a real condition rather than a moral failing.
                  → "the tsundoku had taken over the second
                     bedroom and she had stopped feeling guilty
                     about it."


cafuné            [brazilian portuguese]
                  the act of tenderly running one's fingers through
                  someone's hair.
                  → "the cafuné went on for some minutes and she
                     fell asleep in his lap."


sobremesa         [spanish]
                  the conversation that happens after a meal,
                  sitting at the table, no longer eating, in no
                  hurry to leave. a named social practice rather
                  than just lingering.
                  → "the sobremesa lasted longer than the meal
                     and the meal had been long."


desenrascanço     [portuguese]
                  the art of improvising a solution out of
                  available materials with no resources, no time,
                  and no clear path. cultural keyword in Portuguese
                  identity.
                  → "the engineer's whole career was a long
                     desenrascanço and the company never figured
                     out how he kept things working."


dépaysement       [french]
                  the feeling of not being in one's home country;
                  the disorientation of being out of place in a
                  way that is not unpleasant but is not nothing.
                  → "the dépaysement on the second day of the
                     trip was the part of the trip he had paid
                     for, in retrospect."


l'esprit          [french, "the spirit of the staircase"]
de l'escalier     the perfect retort that arrives only after the
                  conversation has ended. named after thinking of
                  the right thing to say while walking down the
                  stairs from the dinner party.
                  → "the l'esprit de l'escalier hit him on the
                     drive home and he gripped the steering wheel
                     until his knuckles whitened."


abbiocco          [italian]
                  the heavy, pleasant drowsiness that follows a
                  large meal.
                  → "the abbiocco took the table at three in the
                     afternoon and nobody got up for an hour."


tarab             [arabic, طرب]
                  the ecstatic state of musical absorption; being
                  carried by music to a place beyond ordinary
                  consciousness. specific category in Arab musical
                  tradition.
                  → "the oud player took the room into tarab and
                     the rest of the night was an aftermath."


gigil             [tagalog]
                  the irresistible urge to pinch or squeeze
                  something cute. acknowledged as a real impulse
                  with its own token rather than a quirk to be
                  apologized for.
                  → "the gigil hit when she saw the puppy and
                     she pretended she did not have it."


apricity          [english, archaic]
                  the warmth of the sun in winter. note: english
                  did have this token, in the seventeenth century.
                  english stopped using it. english then forgot
                  the feeling existed as a nameable thing. the
                  loss of an English token is itself a tokenization
                  failure worth observing.
                  → "the apricity on the bench at noon was the
                     reason she still came back to the park
                     between treatments."


petrichor         [english, modern coinage from greek roots]
                  the smell of rain on dry earth. coined by two
                  australian scientists, Bear and Thomas, in 1964.
                  english did not have this word until sixty years
                  ago. the smell is older. note: tokens can be
                  added at any time. this is what installing a new
                  token looks like.
                  → "the petrichor came up off the asphalt and he
                     remembered every summer at once."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
NOTE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Two tokens above are deliberately included to illustrate two ends of
the same mechanic:

   - APRICITY shows that English HAD a token, lost it, and
     subsequently lost the ability to recognize the feeling as
     a nameable thing. The loss of a token is the deletion of
     a thought.

   - PETRICHOR shows that English DID NOT HAVE a token until 1964,
     gained one, and English speakers can now perceive the smell
     as a discrete recognizable feature of the world. The addition
     of a token is the construction of a thought.

The dictionary is editable. Most of the editing is done to you. Some
of the editing is done by you. The asymmetry is the work.
